Computer searches

Computer searches have counted still-lifes and pseudo-still-lifes up to 24 bits (by Raynham, Buckingham, and Niemiec and Koenig), period 2 oscillators and pseudo-oscillators up to 21 bits (by Niemiec and Koenig), period 3 oscillators and pseudo-oscillators up to 20 bits (by Beluchenko), and all objects of all types up to 10 bits (by Thompson).

Such lists of objects can also yield robust hand-assembled lists of pseudo-objects, since their assembly typically involves fairly trivial combinatorical procedures.

Numbers in bold face have been confirmed by computer search (or trivial hand-assembly). Other given numbers are believed to be complete, but have not yet been verified. Lists with large numbers of objects that have not yet been counted are shown with "many".

Status of object lists on sub-pages is shown by background color:

  • White (the default) indicates a list where all items are counted and shown, and syntheses exist (or at least have been attempted) for all. (Light blue is similar, and used to make table structure easier to read.)
  • Blue is like white, except syntheses for all are planned, but are not yet complete.
  • Yellow indicates a list of up to 10000 items where all items have been counted and shown, but for which there is no attempt to provide syntheses for all items. Lists of more than 1000 items show much smaller objects, with 1000 per page rather than 100.
  • Green is like yellow, except all items have also been analyzed to determine whether syntheses are possible, and trivial syntheses exist for most. For the few that do not have trivial syntheses, explicit syntheses have been developed for all (or at least most).
  • Red indicates a list of items where all items have been counted, but are not shown. This is typically for objects with more than 10000 items, or for hand-generated lists that are much easier to enumerate than to instantiate (here, typically objects in the 22-25 bit range).
  • Orange is like red, except all items have also been analyzed to determine whether syntheses are possible, and trivial syntheses exist for most. For the few that do not have trivial syntheses, explicit syntheses have been developed for all (or at least most).
  • Grey indicates a list of items where no definitive item lists exists, either because a computer search would exceed available computing resources, or there are too many items to count by hand.
  • Purple is like grey, except all known item categories have also been analyzed to determine whether syntheses are possible, and trivial syntheses exist for most. For the few that do not have trivial syntheses, explicit syntheses have been developed for all (or at least most).
  • Teal indicates an aggregate list, containing the sums of related lists preceding them.
  • In all but the first two cases, these may be accompanied by smaller lists of selected objects for which syntheses have been found, but these are haphazard rather than systematic.
  • Lists that have been verified by computer search (or smaller manually-generated lists of pseudo-objects based on verified lists of objects) are shown in boldface.

Spaceships

Most of the known Life spaceships (at least the small ones) move at a velocity of c/2. These have a base period of 4, but some of the larger variants have periods that are multiples of this.

H. Koenig's computer searches have not revealed any spaceships that were not previously known, but they have confirmed that the previously-known lists of c/2 spaceships have been complete up to 24 bits, as well as smaller limits for other velocities. In 2013, Josh Ball added the loafer to this list.
